bring
v
Grammar
bring (third-person singular simple present brings, present participle bringing, simple past brought, past participle brought or (rare, dialectal) broughten)
Etymology
From Middle English bryngen, from Old English bringan, from Proto-West Germanic *bringan, from Proto-Germanic *bringaną (“to bring”), from Proto-Indo-European *bʰrenk-, possibly based on *bʰer-. Compare Scots bring, West Frisian bringe, Low German brengen, Dutch brengen, Afrikaans bring, German bringen; also Welsh hebrwng (“to bring, lead”), Tocharian B pränk- (“to take away; restrain oneself, hold back”), Latvian brankti (“lying close”), Lithuanian branktas (“whiffletree”).
  1. vdt vt
    To transport toward somebody/somewhere.
    1 example
    Waiter, please bring me a single malt whiskey.
  2. vt fig
    To supply or contribute.
    1 example
    The new company director brought a fresh perspective on sales and marketing.
  3. vt
    To occasion or bring about.
    3 examples
    Let's bring our differences to an issue.
    The controversial TV broadcast brought a storm of complaints.
    Seeing her brought the memories flooding back and tears to my eyes.
  4. vt
    To raise (a lawsuit, charges, etc.) against somebody.
  5. To persuade; to induce; to draw; to lead; to guide.
    1 example
    It seems so preposterous a thing[…]that they do not easily bring themselves to it.
  6. To produce in exchange; to sell for; to fetch.
    1 example
    What does coal bring per ton?
  7. sports
    To pitch, often referring to a particularly hard thrown fastball.
    1 example
    The closer Jones can really bring it.
  8. chess
    To move a piece into a more active position, esp. to initially develop it.
    1 example
    You want to bring the rook and then start pushing your pawns.

participate
v
Grammar
participate (third-person singular simple present participates, present participle participating, simple past and past participle participated)
Etymology
Borrowed from Latin participātus, the perfect passive participle of participō (“to take part in, share in, give part in, impart”) (see -ate (verb-forming suffix) and -ate (adjective-forming suffix)), from particeps (“taking part (in), sharing (in); someone who takes part (in)”, particip- in compounds), from pars (“part”, part- in compounds) + -ceps (“which takes, taker”), literally “(someone) who takes part”; see part and capable. Compare Old English dǣlniman (“to participate”), an earlier calque of the same Latin verb.
  1. vi
    To join in, to take part, to involve oneself (in something).
  2. vt obs
    To share, to take part in (something).
    1 example
    A spirit I am indeed; But am in that dimension grossly clad Which from the womb I did participate .
  3. obs
    To share (something) with others; to transfer (something) to or unto others.

peninsular
n
Grammar
peninsular (plural peninsulars or peninsulares)
Etymology
Learned borrowing from Latin paenīnsulāris. By surface analysis, peninsula + -ar. In the historical sense borrowed from Spanish peninsular.
  1. One who inhabits a peninsula.
  2. hist
    A person born on the Iberian Peninsula who emigrated to a (contemporary or former) Spanish colony.
